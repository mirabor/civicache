    3. Subsection "Hypothesized mechanism: SIEVE's visited-bit as a poor man's admission filter" (~250 words). Adapted from 05-ANALYSIS.md lines 124-148. Key claims:

    - SIEVE's visited-bit eviction is functionally similar to TinyLFU's admission filter, discovered through a different implementation path.
    - On a miss, a new key enters the cache with visited=false. On a hit, the bit flips true. Eviction scans forward, clearing visited=true bits and skipping, evicting the first visited=false key.
    - **Effectively, SIEVE evicts one-hit wonders aggressively.** That is functionally similar to TinyLFU's "admit only if frequency ≥ victim."
    - On Congress (α_raw=0.231, uniform sizes, 91.7% unique keys): the raw trace gives almost no frequency signal. W-TinyLFU's CMS sees essentially-all-ones counters (most keys seen once or twice). The admission filter becomes close to a no-op. Meanwhile SIEVE's visited-bit is equally effective at rejecting OHWs after one cache-cycle. The two policies converge.
    - On Court (α_raw=1.028, long-tail sizes, real locality): the raw trace has real structure. W-TinyLFU's CMS captures a *gradient* of frequencies — key A seen 5 times, B seen 2 times, C once. Admission filter preserves hot keys decisively. SIEVE's *binary* visited-bit loses this gradient — a key seen once and a key seen fifty times are indistinguishable to the scanner. Hence W-TinyLFU pulls ahead by ~5pp.

    Cite `\cite{zhang2024sieve}` and `\cite{einziger2017tinylfu}` in this subsection.

    4. Subsection "Addendum: does the ordering invert at higher α?" (~150 words). Adapted from 05-ANALYSIS.md lines 397-425 (the Phase 5 addendum). We extended the α grid on Congress to {1.3, 1.4, 1.5} with a single-seed run. Result: the crossover hypothesis partially holds — W-TinyLFU edges SIEVE by 0.0008 at α ∈ {1.4, 1.5}, but this is below the 5-seed σ ≈ 0.007-0.010 at these α values. The stronger conclusion: SIEVE and W-TinyLFU are functionally equivalent on Congress across the entire α range from 0.6 to 1.5. Quote the paper claim verbatim from 05-ANALYSIS.md lines 434-442:

    > "On Congress (α_raw=0.23, near-uniform raw trace, uniform object sizes), SIEVE's visited-bit is functionally equivalent to W-TinyLFU's Count-Min Sketch across α ∈ [0.6, 1.5]. On Court (α_raw=1.03, long-tail sizes, real raw locality), W-TinyLFU's frequency gradient captures information SIEVE's binary signal cannot. Choice of admission mechanism matters *only* when the raw workload provides a frequency gradient to exploit."

    3. Subsection "The mechanism" (~150 words) — adapted from 05-ANALYSIS.md lines 189-205. Court's 462,490-byte max is 335× the 1,381-byte median. That single outlier represents:
    - 0.78% of the working set by bytes (462KB / 59.6MB)
    - 0.007% of the objects (1 / 15,018)

    At cache_frac=0.01 on Court (working set 59.6MB), the cache is ~600KB. That single outlier is ~77% of the cache. Whether it lives in cache for most of the trace is a near-binary signal on byte-miss-ratio. The replay-Zipf seed controls which key gets the highest popularity rank. If the seed assigns the outlier rank-1 (highest popularity), it stays cached → low byte-miss. If the seed assigns rank-10000+, it never enters cache → high byte-miss. No eviction policy can fix this; it's a popularity-assignment artifact.

    Practitioner takeaway: byte-miss benchmarks on traces with long tails can be misleading unless CI bands are reported. Multi-seed CI bands are essential for heavy-tailed real-world workloads, not just a matter of statistical rigor.
