\section{Why SIEVE Ties W-TinyLFU on Congress (and Loses on Court)}\label{sec:mechanism}

The most interesting finding this paper produced contradicts the TinyLFU
paper's narrative that W-TinyLFU dominates on skewed traces. On Congress, the
W-TinyLFU vs.\ SIEVE miss-ratio gap is always within $\pm 0.005$ (less than
$1\sigma$) across $\alpha \in \{0.6, 0.7, \dots, 1.5\}$ --- the two policies are
statistically tied. On Court, W-TinyLFU wins by a stable $4$--$5$pp at every
$\alpha$. The explanation is not about the policies. It is about the
workloads: the admission-filter benefit is gated by whether the raw trace
carries a frequency gradient above what SIEVE's visited-bit already captures.

\subsection{Hypothesized mechanism: SIEVE's visited-bit as a poor-man's admission filter}

SIEVE's visited-bit eviction is \emph{functionally similar} to TinyLFU's
admission filter, discovered through a different implementation path
\cite{zhang2024sieve,einziger2017tinylfu}. On a miss, a new key enters the
cache with \texttt{visited=false}. On a hit, the bit flips to \texttt{true}.
The eviction scanner advances a hand pointer, clearing \texttt{visited=true}
bits (and skipping those keys), evicting the first \texttt{visited=false} key
it hits. Effectively, \textbf{SIEVE evicts one-hit wonders aggressively} --- a
key that was admitted but never re-accessed is the primary eviction target.
That is mechanistically close to TinyLFU's ``admit only if estimated frequency
$\geq$ victim frequency'' rule, just expressed with one bit per key instead of
a Count-Min Sketch (CMS).

\textbf{On Congress ($\alpha_{\text{raw}}=0.231$, uniform object sizes, 91.7\%
unique keys; see Table~\ref{tab:workload}):} the raw trace provides almost no
frequency signal. W-TinyLFU's CMS sees essentially-all-ones counters (most keys
are seen once or twice over the trace). The admission filter collapses to
near-no-op behavior. Meanwhile SIEVE's visited-bit is equally effective at
rejecting one-hit wonders after a single cache cycle. The two policies
converge because the information content they extract converges: both are
reduced to ``has this key been seen more than once?'' --- a binary signal.

\textbf{On Court ($\alpha_{\text{raw}}=1.028$, long-tail object sizes, real raw
locality):} the raw trace carries real structure. W-TinyLFU's CMS captures a
\emph{gradient} of frequencies --- key $A$ has been seen 5 times, $B$ has been
seen 2 times, $C$ once. The admission filter preserves hot keys decisively.
SIEVE's \emph{binary} visited-bit loses this gradient: a key seen once and a
key seen fifty times are indistinguishable to the scanner. W-TinyLFU pulls
ahead by the stable $\sim 5$pp recorded in Table~\ref{tab:mechanism_gap}.

\subsection{Addendum: does the ordering invert at higher $\alpha$?}

The natural follow-up question is whether Congress's $\alpha_{\text{raw}}=0.23$
is simply too weak, and whether a stronger Zipf overlay would eventually
surface a frequency gradient W-TinyLFU could exploit. We extended the Congress
$\alpha$ grid to $\{1.3, 1.4, 1.5\}$ with a \emph{single-seed} run (the
multi-seed infrastructure stops at 1.2 by design; rerunning it is deferred to
v2). The result: the crossover hypothesis \emph{partially} holds ---
W-TinyLFU edges SIEVE by $+0.0008$ at $\alpha \in \{1.4, 1.5\}$, but this is
well below the 5-seed $\sigma \approx 0.007$--$0.010$ at these $\alpha$ values.
The stronger conclusion is that \textbf{SIEVE and W-TinyLFU are functionally
equivalent on Congress across the entire usable $\alpha$ range from $0.6$ to
$1.5$}. The single-seed hedge is explicit; a CI-backed follow-up is listed in
\S\ref{sec:conclusion} as V2-04.

The paper claim, quoted from the Phase~5 analysis:

\begin{quote}\itshape\small
On Congress ($\alpha_{\text{raw}}=0.23$, near-uniform raw trace, uniform object
sizes), SIEVE's visited-bit is functionally equivalent to W-TinyLFU's
Count-Min Sketch across $\alpha \in [0.6, 1.5]$. On Court
($\alpha_{\text{raw}}=1.03$, long-tail sizes, real raw locality), W-TinyLFU's
frequency gradient captures information SIEVE's binary signal cannot. Choice
of admission mechanism matters \emph{only} when the raw workload provides a
frequency gradient to exploit.
\end{quote}

\subsection{What this means for the TinyLFU narrative}

The TinyLFU paper's ``W-TinyLFU wins on Zipf'' framing \cite{einziger2017tinylfu}
implicitly assumes the Zipf overlay is the whole story. Our data shows that
\emph{raw trace structure matters independently of the $\alpha$ overlay}: on
Congress, no accessible $\alpha$ produces a frequency gradient rich enough for
the CMS to beat a visited-bit; on Court, even modest $\alpha$ values surface
structure W-TinyLFU can exploit. This is consistent with the Caffeine
project's production decision to omit the Doorkeeper from its default
W-TinyLFU configuration \cite{caffeine2024} --- in high-locality production
traces, the frequency signal is rich enough that further admission hedges are
not net-helpful; in low-locality traces, no amount of admission filtering
extracts signal that is not there.
